Heuristics ($h(n)$) are domain-specific functions that estimate the cost from a given state to the nearest goal. Their mathematical properties determine the efficiency and optimality of informed search algorithms.

### 1. Admissibility
A heuristic is **Admissible** if it never overestimates the true cost to reach the goal.
* **Formal Definition**: $0 \le h(n) \le h^*(n)$, where $h^*(n)$ is the true optimal cost from $n$ to the goal.
* **Significance**: Admissibility is a sufficient condition for [[A-Star]] to be optimal in **Tree Search**.

### 2. Consistency (Monotonicity)
A heuristic is **Consistent** if, for every node $n$ and every successor $n'$ generated by an action $a$:
* **Formal Definition**: $h(n) \le COST(n, a, n') + h(n')$.
* **Triangle Inequality**: The estimated cost to the goal from $n$ is no greater than the step cost to $n'$ plus the estimated cost from $n'$.
* **Significance**: Consistency is required to guarantee optimality for [[A-Star]] in **Graph Search**, ensuring that the first time a state is reached, it is via the optimal path.

### 3. Dominance
If we have two admissible heuristics $h_1$ and $h_2$, and $h_2(n) \ge h_1(n)$ for all $n$:
* **Definition**: $h_2$ **dominates** $h_1$.
* **Efficiency**: A dominating heuristic is "more informed" and will always result in fewer (or equal) node expansions than the heuristic it dominates.
