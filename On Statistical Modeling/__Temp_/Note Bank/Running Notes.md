### 1. The Simplified Representation

We divide both sides of the convergence by $\sigma$:

$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$

Alternatively, you often see the $\sqrt{n}$ moved to the denominator of the denominator to show the **Standard Error**:

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$

### 2. Why this is the "Exam Version"

In your lecture notes (Section 2.3) and the mock exams, you rarely know the true $\sigma$. Therefore, you use the **Slutsky Theorem** (found in your `Lecture_Notes_14_05.pdf` Appendix) to replace the unknown $\sigma$ with the sample standard deviation $S_n$.

Because $S_n$ converges to $\sigma$, the relationship still holds:

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{S_n / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$

### 3. Comparison of the components

|**Component**|**Meaning**|
|---|---|
|**$\bar{X}_n - \mu$**|The **Distance**: How far your sample average is from the truth.|
|**$\sigma / \sqrt{n}$**|The **Standard Error**: The "expected" wiggle room for an average of size $n$.|
|**The Ratio**|The **Z-score**: How many "units of noise" your result is away from the truth.|

In a one-sample $t$-test, the "Null Hypothesis" ($H_0$) is usually the skeptical assumption that the true average of a population is exactly equal to some specific value (let's call it $\mu_0$).

When you run the test and get your result, the decision to "Reject" or "Fail to Reject" is the final verdict of your experiment. Here is what those two outcomes actually mean in plain English.

### 1. Rejecting the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$)

**The Plain English Meaning:** "We found something significant. The difference we see is too big to be explained by random luck alone."

- **The Logic:** Your sample mean was so far away from the assumed $\mu_0$ that it landed in the "rejection region" (the extreme tails) of the $t$-distribution.
    
- **The Conclusion:** You have enough evidence to claim that the population mean is likely **not** $\mu_0$.
    
- **The Risk:** There is a small chance (usually 5%, defined by your $\alpha$) that you are wrong and the result was just a very rare fluke. This is called a **Type I Error**.
    

### 2. Failing to Reject the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$)

**The Plain English Meaning:** "We didn't find anything special. Any difference we saw could easily just be normal, random 'noise' in the data."

- **The Logic:** Your sample mean was relatively close to $\mu_0$. It landed in the "fat middle" of the $t$-distribution, where we expect 95% of random samples to land.
    
- **The Conclusion:** You do **not** have enough evidence to say the mean is different from $\mu_0$.
    
- **Crucial Note:** This does _not_ prove the Null Hypothesis is true. It just means your data isn't strong enough to prove it's false. (Think of a "Not Guilty" verdict in court: it doesn't mean the person is innocent; it just means there wasn't enough evidence to convict.)
    
- **The Risk:** There is a chance that the mean actually _is_ different, but your sample was too small or too messy to notice. This is called a **Type II Error**.
    

---

### How to decide? (The P-Value vs. Alpha)

To make this decision in your exercises (like in `Exercise1.pdf` or `Mock_exam.pdf`), you compare your **p-value** to your **significance level ($\alpha$)**:

- **If p-value < $\alpha$:** The result is "surprising." **Reject $H_0$.**
    
- **If p-value $\ge$ $\alpha$:** The result is "boring." **Fail to Reject $H_0$.**
    

### Application to your Linear Models

In the context of the **Gaussian Linear Model** from your notes:

- If you are testing a coefficient $\gamma_1$, the Null Hypothesis is usually $H_0: \gamma_1 = 0$ (meaning the variable has no effect).
    
- **Rejecting $H_0$** means you've proven that variable $X_1$ actually matters and has a real relationship with $Y$.
    
- **Failing to reject $H_0$** means $X_1$ might as well be zero; it isn't helping your model predict anything.

The labels $t_{2.5\%}$ and $t_{97.5\%}$ are **quantiles**. A quantile tells you the specific "address" on the x-axis where a certain percentage of the total area lies to its **left**.

Here is why the right side is labeled 97.5%:

### 1. Cumulative Area (The "Left-to-Right" Rule)

In statistics, percentiles and quantiles are always calculated starting from the far left ($-\infty$).

- **The Left Boundary ($t_{2.5\%}$):** If you start at the left wall and move right, you stop when you have covered exactly **2.5%** of the total area. That point is your "2.5th percentile."
    
- **The Right Boundary ($t_{97.5\%}$):** You continue moving right, passing through the massive blue middle. To leave exactly **2.5%** in the right-hand red tail, you must have already passed through **97.5%** of the total area ($100\% - 2.5\% = 97.5\%$).
    

So yes, you are exactly right: the "address" $t_{97.5\%}$ represents the **blue area + the left red area**.

### 2. Symmetry of the $t$-Distribution

Because the $t$-distribution is perfectly symmetrical around zero, these two addresses are actually the same distance from the center, just with opposite signs:

- If $t_{97.5\%}$ is **2.04**, then $t_{2.5\%}$ is **-2.04**.
    
- In your exam formulas (like in your `Mock_exam.pdf`), you will often see this written as $t_{1-\alpha/2}$. For a 5% level ($\alpha = 0.05$), $1 - 0.025 = 0.975$.
    

### 3. The "Critical Region" vs. The "Confidence Level"

The diagram is showing a **two-tailed test**:

- **The Confidence Level (95%):** This is the blue area in the middle. It represents the range of "normal" results where we fail to reject $H_0$.
    
- **The Significance Level / Alpha (5%):** This is the total red area. Because it is two-tailed, we split that 5% into two "danger zones" of **2.5% each**.

Here is the step-by-step explainer of the theoretical flow mapping how hypothesis tests and confidence intervals are two sides of the exact same mathematical coin.

### 1. The Algebraic Pivot (Building the Interval)

The logical flow starts with a simple probability statement about the test statistic $T$ under the Neyman-Pearson framework. We know that 95% of the time, our test statistic will fall in the "safe" middle zone between our two critical boundaries:

$$P(t_{2.5\%} \le T \le t_{97.5\%}) = 0.95$$

The genius of the confidence interval is treating this probability statement as an algebraic equation. By expanding $T$ into its actual formula for a two-sample difference of means, we get:

$$P\left(t_{2.5\%} \le \frac{(\bar{X}_B - \bar{X}_A) - (\mu_B - \mu_A)}{\sigma_T} \le t_{97.5\%}\right) = 0.95$$

Through basic algebra—multiplying by the standard error $\sigma_T$, and then subtracting the observed difference of means $(\bar{X}_B - \bar{X}_A)$—we "pivot" the inequality. We isolate the unknown population parameter $(\mu_B - \mu_A)$ in the absolute center.

$$P((\bar{X}_B - \bar{X}_A) - \sigma_T t_{97.5\%} \le (\mu_B - \mu_A) \le (\bar{X}_B - \bar{X}_A) + \sigma_T t_{97.5\%}) = 0.95$$

Notice what happened: the population parameter in the middle is completely static. The values on the _outside_ (the endpoints) contain $\bar{X}_B$, $\bar{X}_A$, and $\sigma_T$. Because these are calculated from our randomly drawn sample, **the endpoints themselves are random variables**.

### 2. The Trap: Why isn't this the probability of the population difference?

You asked why our data "telling us what values could have produced the data" is NOT the same as "giving a probability for each population difference."

This touches on the strict philosophical foundation of **Frequentist Statistics** (which governs this entire framework).

- **The Population Parameter is a Fact:** The true difference between the populations ($\mu_B - \mu_A$) is a fixed, immutable physical reality. It is not moving. It is not a random variable. Therefore, it does not have a "probability distribution."
    
- **The Interval is the Net:** Because the endpoints of our interval are random variables, the interval acts like a net we throw.
    
- **The Reality:** When we say "95% Confidence," we are strictly saying: _If we repeated this experiment infinitely many times and calculated the interval the exact same way, 95% of those randomly generated nets would successfully capture the fixed parameter._
    

Once you calculate a specific interval with your specific dataset (e.g., $[2.1, 5.4]$), the true parameter is either inside that specific interval, or it isn't. The probability is exactly 1 or 0. We just don't know which.

### 3. The Duality (Confidence vs. Alpha)

Because the confidence interval is built by simply rearranging the exact same inequality used for the hypothesis test, they are perfectly mechanically linked.

- The **Significance Level ($\alpha$)** is the area we cut off at the tails (e.g., $\alpha = 0.05$).
    
- The **Confidence Level** is the area remaining in the middle ($1 - \alpha = 0.95$).
    

If we hypothesize a specific value (like testing $H_0: \mu_B - \mu_A = 0$), and that hypothesized value of $0$ falls _inside_ our 95% confidence interval, it means our test statistic $T$ safely landed in the 95% middle zone. We **fail to reject the null hypothesis**. If $0$ is outside the interval, it means $T$ landed in the 5% red tails. We **reject the null hypothesis**.

### Textual Representation of the Neyman-Pearson Graph

**Visual Layout:** A symmetrical bell curve representing the probability density function $p(t)$ of the test statistic under the assumption that the Null Hypothesis ($H_0$) is true.

**The Middle (Blue Area):** The central, vast majority of the curve is shaded blue. This represents the 95% probability zone where the test statistic is expected to land if the null hypothesis is true.

**The Tails (Red Areas):** The far-left and far-right tails are shaded red. These are the critical regions (or rejection regions).

**The Boundaries:** The line separating the left red tail from the blue center is labeled with the 2.5th percentile mark ($t_{2.5\%}$), cutting off exactly 2.5% of the area. The line separating the right red tail from the blue center is labeled with the 97.5th percentile mark ($t_{97.5\%}$), cutting off the remaining 2.5% of the area. Together, these tails sum to the 5% significance level ($\alpha$).

Theconsideration for the one-sided alternative is also important
The distinction between a **two-sided** and a **one-sided** alternative lies entirely in how you define your "direction of interest" before you even look at the data.

In your `\Definitions\Hypothesis Testing.md` notes, this is the difference between looking for **any** change versus looking for a **specific** change.

---

### 1. Two-Sided Alternative ($H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$)

**The Goal:** You want to know if the population mean is _different_ from your null value. You don’t care if it’s much higher or much lower—both are equally "surprising" to you.

- **The Critical Region:** Because you are open to surprises in both directions, you split your risk ($\alpha$). You put half ($\alpha/2$) in the left tail and half ($\alpha/2$) in the right tail.
    
- **The Boundaries:** As we discussed with the 97.5% quantile, your "rejection zones" are far to the left and far to the right.
    
- **Example:** Testing if a new medicine changes blood pressure. You’d be interested if it raises it (dangerous) or lowers it (the goal).
    

### 2. One-Sided Alternative ($H_1: \mu > \mu_0$ or $H_1: \mu < \mu_0$)

**The Goal:** You have a specific theory that the mean can only move in one direction, or you only _care_ if it moves in that direction.

- **The Critical Region:** You put your entire risk budget ($\alpha$) into **one single tail**.
    
- **The Boundaries:** If $\alpha = 0.05$, your boundary point is the 95th percentile ($t_{95\%}$), not the 97.5th. This makes the boundary "closer" to the center, making it **easier to reject the null**—but only if the data moves in your predicted direction.
    
- **The Risk:** If you test $H_1: \mu > \mu_0$ and the data comes back with a massive negative difference, you **cannot** reject the null, no matter how "significant" that negative result looks. You’ve bet everything on the right tail.

### Extension for your "Duality" Note

This distinction changes your **Confidence Intervals**:

- A **Two-Sided** test corresponds to a standard Confidence Interval with two endpoints $[L, U]$.
    
- A **One-Sided** test corresponds to a "Confidence Bound." For $H_1: \mu > \mu_0$, you would calculate a lower bound $[L, \infty)$. You are effectively saying, "I am 95% sure the true value is at least $L$ or higher."
